# Analyzing-Cardiovascular-Health-Outcomes-Across-American-Communities-Using-X

Objectives: Periodic public health surveillance of cardiovascular health (CVH) is essential for understanding the health of U.S. communities. This study evaluates the feasibility of using Twitter/X data to assess modifiable CVH behaviors, focusing on four Life’s Essential 8™ (LE8) behaviors—Eat Better, Be More Active, Quit Tobacco, Get Healthy Sleep—and two additional behaviors (alcohol and substance use), and examines associations with community-level outcomes.

Setting and Design: 
We analyzed 32 million U.S.-based Twitter/X posts (2017–2022) containing CVH-related keywords and linked county-level tweet-derived signals to health outcomes from PLACES and USDA datasets across U.S. communities defined by American Communities Project (ACP). Analyses included multilevel modeling of tweet-derived behaviors and CVH outcomes, topic modeling of community-specific CVH discourse, models predicting CVH outcomes from tweets across ACP communities, and tests of differences in tweet distributions.
 
Primary and Secondary Outcome measures: Primary outcomes were county-level CVH measures from PLACES and USDA datasets; secondary outcomes included topic patterns in CVH-related tweets.

Results: Multilevel models revealed significant associations between tweet-derived signals and CVH outcomes, with stronger effects for Be More Active (β = −5.59, p < 0.001), Get Healthy Sleep (β = 9.72, p < 0.001), and alcohol use (β = 1.96, p = 0.006). Statistically significant topics were identified in six of fourteen ACP communities, primarily related to smoking and alcohol. Predictive models showed strong performance for Be More Active (r = 0.84) and Quit Tobacco (r = 0.76), and Get Healthy Sleep (r = 0.58) (all p < 0.001). Differences in tweet distributions across communities were significant (p < 0.05).

Conclusions: Social media data like Twitter/X can provide an informative supplement for monitoring modifiable CVH across U.S. communities. Tweet-derived signals were associated with survey-based measures and showed strong predictive performance for physical activity, tobacco use, and sleep.
